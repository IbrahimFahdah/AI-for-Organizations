# Chapter 13: Managing AI Adoption and Change

Deploying AI is a technology project. Adopting AI is an organizational change project. They require different skills, different timelines, and different success metrics.

---

## Change Management Frameworks

Established change management frameworks apply to AI deployments:

- **Prosci ADKAR** — Awareness, Desire, Knowledge, Ability, Reinforcement
- **Kotter 8-Step** — Coalition, vision, communication, short-term wins, sustain

The specific framework matters less than having one. The common failure is treating AI deployment as complete when the system goes live, rather than when people are using it effectively.

---

## Workforce Communication

Communicate before, during, and after deployment.

**Before:** What is being deployed, why, and what it means for each role. Be specific about what changes and what does not.

**During:** Progress updates, early results, how feedback is being handled.

**After:** What the results show, what is being adjusted, what comes next.

Generic reassurance ("AI will help everyone work better") erodes trust. Role-specific honesty builds it.

---

## AI Training Programs

Training must match the actual work, not the vendor's demo. Effective AI training:

- Is role-specific (what this person will actually use AI for)
- Covers failure modes (when not to trust the output)
- Includes hands-on practice with real tasks
- Has a refresher cycle as the system evolves

A one-time 45-minute onboarding is not a training program.

---

## Adoption Barriers

| Barrier | Signal | Response |
|---|---|---|
| Fear of job replacement | Avoidance, minimal use | Honest messaging; connect AI to reduced drudgery |
| Tool is harder to use than not using it | Workarounds | UX improvement; reduce friction |
| Distrust of outputs | Manual verification of everything | Training on when to trust; show accuracy data |
| No time to learn | "Too busy" | Protected time; manager support |
| No incentive to change | Adoption stays flat | Connect to performance objectives |

---

## Building AI Champions

Champions are experienced users who help peers adopt AI. They are not AI experts — they are trusted colleagues who use the tool and can answer practical questions.

Invest in champions:
- Early access and deeper training
- Time to support colleagues
- Recognition for their role
- A channel to escalate product feedback

Champions accelerate adoption more cost-effectively than formal training alone.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Adoption ends when people use the tool effectively, not when it goes live.
- Role-specific communication and training outperform generic programs.
- Champions accelerate peer adoption more than top-down mandates.

**Common mistake:** Measuring adoption by license activation counts rather than active, effective use.
