# Chapter 10: Translating Strategy into Execution

Strategy defines direction. Execution defines what actually happens. The gap between them is where most AI programs fail.

---

## Converting Vision into Initiatives

A strategic AI vision needs to be broken into funded, staffed, time-bounded initiatives. Each initiative should have:

- A specific business problem it solves
- A measurable success criterion
- A named owner with authority to make decisions
- A realistic timeline (pilots typically 8–16 weeks; production 3–9 months)
- A defined budget including ongoing operational costs

Initiatives without these attributes are ideas, not commitments.

---

## Departmental AI Roadmaps

Each business unit should maintain a 12-month AI roadmap covering:

1. **Current state** — what AI is already in use
2. **Pipeline** — use cases under evaluation or in development
3. **Prioritized backlog** — ranked list of future opportunities
4. **Dependencies** — what is needed from central teams (data, platform, governance)

Roadmaps should be reviewed quarterly and adjusted as priorities shift.

---

## Aligning Teams to Business Outcomes

The failure mode: technology teams optimize for model performance while business teams measure headcount and cost. Alignment requires shared metrics.

Practical approach:
- Define the business outcome first
- Work backward to the AI system metric that predicts it
- Include both in team objectives

---

## Defining Measurable Objectives

Use outcome-based objectives, not output-based:

| Output-based (weak) | Outcome-based (strong) |
|---|---|
| Deploy AI chatbot by Q2 | Reduce tier-1 support volume by 30% by Q3 |
| Fine-tune a model on our data | Reduce document review time from 4h to 1h |
| Implement RAG system | 80% of policy questions answered without escalation |

---

## Key Takeaways

- Initiatives without owners, timelines, and success criteria do not get executed.
- Departmental roadmaps prevent duplicate effort and surface shared dependencies.
- Align technology metrics to business outcomes from day one.

**Common mistake:** Treating deployment as the finish line. Deployment is the beginning of the operational phase.
