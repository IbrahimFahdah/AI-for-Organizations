# Organizational Readiness

## Overview

Most AI initiatives fail not because the technology is wrong, but because the organization is not ready to receive it. Readiness is a precondition, not an afterthought.

---

## Readiness Dimensions

### 1. Data Readiness

AI systems depend on data. Before evaluating AI tools, assess:

- Is relevant data collected consistently?
- Is it accessible to the teams who need it?
- Is it clean, labeled, and documented?
- Are there legal constraints on its use (privacy, retention, jurisdiction)?

**Common gap:** Organizations discover during AI projects that their data is siloed, inconsistently formatted, or legally restricted.

### 2. Process Readiness

AI augments or replaces specific steps in a workflow. For AI to be useful, the surrounding process must be:

- Well-defined enough to identify where AI fits
- Stable enough to measure before/after performance
- Flexible enough to be changed when AI is introduced

**Common gap:** AI is introduced into broken or undefined processes, which amplifies dysfunction rather than reducing it.

### 3. People Readiness

Adoption depends on the people doing the work. Assess:

- Do staff understand what the AI tool does and does not do?
- Is there training that matches actual job tasks?
- Are incentives aligned — does using AI help or threaten workers' positions?
- Is there a clear escalation path when AI outputs are wrong?

**Common gap:** Staff distrust AI tools they don't understand, or over-trust them without knowing their limits.

### 4. Leadership Readiness

Leadership readiness means:

- Executives who can articulate what problem AI is solving
- Budget holders who understand multi-year timelines
- Decision-makers willing to act on AI output within defined risk tolerances
- Sponsors who protect projects from being killed before they produce results

**Common gap:** AI projects start with executive enthusiasm and end with loss of interest before ROI materializes.

### 5. Governance Readiness

Governance readiness means having answers to:

- Who is accountable when AI produces a bad outcome?
- What is the review process for AI-generated outputs before they are used?
- How are decisions made about which AI systems are approved for use?
- How are risks monitored over time?

**Common gap:** Governance is designed after deployment, when fixing problems is harder.
