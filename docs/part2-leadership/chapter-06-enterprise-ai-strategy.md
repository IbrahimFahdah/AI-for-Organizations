# Chapter 6: Building an Enterprise AI Strategy

A strategy answers three questions: where to play, how to win, and what capabilities are required. An AI strategy is no different — it just applies those questions to AI investment.

---

## Enterprise Priorities

Not every AI opportunity is worth pursuing. Prioritize use cases that are:

- **High value** — material impact on cost, revenue, or risk
- **Feasible** — data is available, the problem is well-defined, AI can actually help
- **Aligned** — connected to the organization's strategic priorities

Map your candidate use cases on a value/feasibility matrix. Start in the high-value, high-feasibility quadrant.

## Buy vs Build Decisions

| Situation | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| Standard use case, off-the-shelf solution exists | Buy |
| Proprietary data is the differentiator | Build or fine-tune |
| Speed to market is critical | Buy |
| Deep integration with internal systems required | Build |
| Limited engineering capacity | Buy |

Default to buy for first deployments. Build only where differentiation justifies the cost and maintenance burden.

---

## Vendor and Platform Strategy

Avoid deep dependence on a single vendor. Key principles:

- Evaluate 2-3 vendors before committing
- Build abstraction layers so vendors can be swapped
- Assess switching cost before signing long-term contracts
- Include deprecation notice requirements in contracts (minimum 12 months)

See [Chapter 7](chapter-07-governance-oversight.md) for vendor governance.

---

## Investment Planning

AI has a different investment profile than traditional software:

- Higher upfront cost (infrastructure, integration, training)
- Slower time to value (3-18 months depending on use case)
- Ongoing cost (API consumption, monitoring, maintenance)
- Compounding returns as capability matures

A realistic investment plan includes all four phases: pilot, production, scale, and sustain.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Strategy is about where *not* to invest as much as where to invest.
- Buy-first reduces risk and time-to-value for most organizations.
- Investment planning that ignores ongoing operational cost will produce budget surprises.

**Common mistake:** Budgeting only for the initial build; ignoring monitoring, maintenance, and scale costs.
