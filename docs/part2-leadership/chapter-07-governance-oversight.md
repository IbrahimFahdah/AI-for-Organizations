# Chapter 7: AI Governance and Executive Oversight

Governance is the system that determines which AI systems get deployed, who is accountable for their outcomes, and how risks are managed. Executives who delegate governance entirely to committees without owning accountability create a gap that shows up in incidents.

---

## Governance Structures

Effective AI governance operates at two levels:

**Strategic level** — sets policy, approves high-risk use cases, reviews portfolio
- AI Governance Committee (cross-functional: legal, risk, technology, business)
- Meets quarterly minimum; more frequently during rapid deployment phases

**Operational level** — implements policy, reviews individual deployments, monitors compliance
- AI Risk or Center of Excellence team
- Business unit AI leads

Both levels need defined scope. Strategic-only governance produces policy without enforcement; operational-only governance lacks authority.

---

## Policies and Accountability

Every deployed AI system should have documented answers to:

- What does this system do, and what decisions does it influence?
- Who is the accountable business owner?
- What is the risk classification and what oversight is required?
- How are errors detected and corrected?
- When was this last reviewed?

An AI inventory with these fields is a governance minimum.

---

## Risk Ownership

Governance committees can approve; individuals must be accountable.

The named AI system owner is accountable for:
- Output quality within defined thresholds
- Escalation when thresholds are breached
- Periodic review and revalidation

Diffuse accountability — "the committee approved it" — fails when something goes wrong.

---

## Regulatory Readiness

Questions regulators will ask:
- Can you produce an inventory of AI systems in use?
- What is your process for approving new AI deployments?
- How do you test for bias in consequential decisions?
- What human oversight exists for AI decisions affecting individuals?
- What is your incident response process?

If these are unanswerable today, the governance program is not ready for regulatory scrutiny.

---

## Responsible AI Leadership

Executives set the tone for responsible AI. Visible behaviors that matter:
- Requiring governance review before high-visibility AI deployments
- Taking AI incidents seriously rather than minimizing them
- Funding governance as a capability, not a cost center
- Being publicly honest about AI limitations to staff and customers

---

## Key Takeaways

- Governance needs both strategic authority and operational execution.
- Committees approve; named individuals are accountable.
- An AI inventory is the minimum artifact of a functional governance program.

**Common mistake:** Creating an AI ethics committee that reviews proposals but has no enforcement mechanism.
